Book Cover Process

Strange Case of Dr. Jekyll & Mr. Hyde

Problem / Prompt

The objective of this project was to design a compelling book cover for Strange Case of Dr. Jekyll & Mr. Hyde that visually communicates the novel's central theme of duality. The challenge was to represent two opposing identities — Dr. Jekyll and Mr. Hyde — within a single cohesive composition while maintaining clarity, readability, and strong visual hierarchy at a glance. Additionally, the design needed to balance a Gothic aesthetic with modern readability, ensuring that typography, imagery, and layout worked together effectively. Another constraint was making the cover visually engaging without overcrowding it, while still incorporating key elements such as the title, author name, and supporting text..

Solution / Hypothesis

To address this, I focused on creating a design that visually reinforces the concept of duality and internal conflict through composition, contrast, and typography. I used visual contrast as the primary strategy—balancing light vs. dark elements and structured vs. distorted text—to reflect the opposition between Jekyll and Hyde. The layout emphasizes a clear hierarchy, ensuring the title remains the focal point while still allowing supporting text to be legible and organized. Typography was treated as both a functional and expressive element. The arrangement and styling of the title help reinforce tension and imbalance, subtly mirroring the psychological split of the main character. I also ensured that the text remains readable at different scales, which is critical for a book cover format. The overall composition was kept intentional and controlled to avoid clutter, allowing negative space and alignment to guide the viewer’s eye. This results in a design that is both visually striking and conceptually aligned with the narrative themes of the novel.

Research & Analysis

To inform my design, I researched both the thematic content of Strange Case of Dr. Jekyll & Mr. Hyde and existing visual approaches to Gothic literature covers. I focused on the novel’s core themes — duality, repression, morality vs. impulse, and identity fragmentation — and how these ideas are commonly represented visually. Many interpretations of the story emphasize contrast, transformation, and the coexistence of opposing forces within one individual. I also reviewed existing book covers and poster designs for the novel and similar Gothic works. Common visual patterns included:

  • High contrast lighting (light vs. shadow)
  • Split compositions or mirrored imagery
  • Dark, muted color palettes
  • Serif or distorted typography to evoke a classic yet eerie tone

In addition to thematic research, I looked into typographic hierarchy and layout design principles to ensure readability and clarity. This included studying how professional book covers balance title prominence, author name placement, and supporting text without overwhelming the composition.

From my research, I concluded that the most effective way to communicate the story visually was through contrast and controlled imbalance. The repeated use of split imagery and light/dark contrast in existing designs reinforced that duality is best represented through clear visual opposition, rather than subtle symbolism. However, I also noticed that many designs became overly literal or cluttered, which reduced their impact. Based on this, I decided my design should:

  • Emphasize contrast (light vs. dark, order vs. distortion)
  • Maintain a clean composition to avoid visual overload
  • Use typography not just for readability, but as a conceptual element that reflects instability or tension.

I also determined that hierarchy is critical—the viewer should immediately recognize the title, while secondary information supports rather than competes with it. Overall, my analysis led me to prioritize a design that is visually bold, thematically clear, and structurally organized, allowing the concept of duality to come through without sacrificing readability or professionalism.

Ideation

During the ideation phase, I explored multiple visual directions to represent the theme of duality in Strange Case of Dr. Jekyll & Mr. Hyde. My initial concepts focused on how to visually separate or contrast two identities within a single composition. Some of the early ideas included:

  • A split composition ,where one side represents Dr. Jekyll and the other represents Mr. Hyde through contrasting tone or imagery
  • A layered or overlapping design ,suggesting that both identities exist simultaneously rather than separately
  • Experimentation with typography placement and distortion ,where the title itself reflects instability or transformation
  • Use of dark, atmospheric backgrounds to reinforce the Gothic tone of the story

As I developed these ideas, I tested different layouts and arrangements to see how effectively they communicated the concept while still maintaining readability. Some versions leaned too heavily into visual effects, while others were too minimal and did not fully capture the tension of the narrative. This phase allowed me to explore a range of possibilities before narrowing down the most effective direction.

Iteration

Throughout the design process, I refined my concept based on both feedback and self-evaluation. One of the main areas of iteration was composition and alignment. Early versions of the design had issues with centering and balance, particularly with background elements and the placement of the title. I adjusted these elements to create a more cohesive and visually stable layout. Typography also went through several revisions. I experimented with different sizes, spacing, and arrangements to improve hierarchy and legibility, ensuring that the title remained the focal point while still supporting the overall concept. In some iterations, the text was either too crowded or not visually impactful enough, so I refined it to better balance clarity with expression. I also made adjustments to contrast and visual emphasis. Increasing contrast helped strengthen the theme of duality and made the design more striking from a distance. At the same time, I simplified certain elements to avoid clutter and keep the composition clean. Feedback played a key role in this process, helping me identify areas where the design felt off-balance or unclear. Each revision brought the design closer to a solution that is both conceptually strong and visually effective.

  • My first iteration of the book cover where the intial direction was to shroud the covers illustration in darkness to signify that of a man shrouded in darkness.
  • My second iteration of the book where I utilized the critiques given by my Professor to add more texture to the art work. I changed the alignment and composition of the text and illustration.
  • My final iteration of the book cover. The goal here was to showcase the split duality of man within the illustration, while also upgrading the title’s alignment, hierarchy, weight, and legibility.

Design Principles

In this project, I applied several key design principles to create a visually effective and conceptually strong book cover. Contrast was one of the most important principles used. By incorporating differences in light vs. dark elements and clean vs. distorted forms, I reinforced the theme of duality central to the story. This contrast helps immediately communicate tension and draws the viewer’s attention. Visual Hierarchy was carefully considered to ensure readability. The title is the most prominent element on the cover, followed by the author’s name and supporting text. Scale, placement, and spacing were used to guide the viewer’s eye in a clear and intentional order. Alignment played a key role in maintaining a clean and organized layout. Even with expressive or stylized elements, the underlying structure ensures the design does not feel chaotic or unbalanced. Balance was used to stabilize the composition. While the concept involves opposition and tension, the layout itself remains visually controlled so that the design feels cohesive rather than overwhelming. Typography was treated as both a functional and expressive element. The arrangement and styling of the text contribute to the overall mood while still maintaining legibility. This balance between expression and clarity was essential for an effective book cover design. Lastly, negative space was used strategically to prevent clutter and allow key elements to stand out. This helps improve readability and gives the design a more polished, professional appearance.
